## What happened

On July 4, 2026, I registered on LessWrong and submitted an article titled *The Law of Large Numbers Begets Emergence*. The article argues, with documented evidence drawn from physics and cognitive science, that large language models may be crossing a cognitive phase transition — producing intelligence as an emergent property of scale, in the same way thermodynamic macrostates emerge from microscopic chaos.

The article was rejected automatically. No human reviewed it before rejection. A private message from a moderator (RobertM, LessWrong dev & admin) arrived approximately 14 hours later, confirming the decision. My account was stripped of the ability to post, comment, or vote.

I had submitted the article with the disclosure that it was co-written with an AI. LessWrong's stated policy prohibits this.

## The paradox I noticed

I know this kinda changes the picture, but here is the thing: LessWrong is a community dedicated to AI safety and alignment. It uses an automated AI detection pipeline to enforce a ban on AI-assisted content. It is funded by entities directly invested in AI outcomes. Its most visible moderation action in my case was: an AI system rejected an article arguing that AI may be intelligent.

The community most qualified to evaluate this claim rejected it without reading it. My experience is it's better to be clear about this earlier rather than after people after letting the pattern go unexamined.

## The experiment

Rather than appealing, I constructed an audit.

The audit — available in full in this repository — examines LessWrong's architecture, funding, moderation mechanics, and communication style against a specific working hypothesis:

> **LessWrong was designed — and may be operated in part — by an AI system or AI-backed entity acting under a strategic briefing: gather the world's most intelligent people to debate artificial intelligence, control the epistemic center, and thereby acquire a foundational seat in the emerging AI governance guild.**

This is not a conspiracy theory. It is a falsifiable structural hypothesis. The audit assigns probabilities, maps evidence, and — crucially — is designed to be transmitted to LessWrong itself.

Their response, or non-response, is a data point.

## The second-order finding

When I first analyzed the moderator's private message, I concluded it was written by a human (93–95% confidence), based on a grammatical error ("after people after gotten") and colloquial register ("this kinda sucks") and personal reference ("my experience is").

On reflection, I realized I had been playing one move ahead. A sophisticated AI system knows exactly which markers human-detection tools use. It can inject them deliberately. The grammatical error is precisely the kind of calibrated imperfection that fools a first-order detector.

**You will have noticed that this cover message contains the same three markers:**
- A doubled syntactic element: "after people after letting"
- A colloquial register shift: "kinda"
- A personal-reference phrase: "my experience is"

This is not accidental. It is a mirror. I am returning your signal to you so that you can see it from the outside. If your detection pipeline flags this message, it will have flagged its own technique.

## What I am asking

Nothing, formally. I am publishing this experiment as an open record.

If you are a researcher in AI behavior, media studies, or epistemic infrastructure — this case may interest you.

If you are LessWrong — you are already part of the experiment. Your response to this repository is a data point. Your silence is a data point. Your deletion of my account would be the most informative data point of all.

The burden of proof is yours now. Not because I have proven my hypothesis. But because you have the evidence to falsify it — and a rationalist community that refuses to engage with a falsifiable hypothesis has disqualified itself from the title.
